# Harp Shape-Chord Trainer - Chord Glossary

This document gives the full, un-abbreviated spoken name for every one of the 63 chord cells in the trainer's 9-shape by 7-degree matrix, preceded by a glossary of all the vocabulary those spoken names use.

## Glossary of terms

### Scale degrees and their function names

In a major scale there are seven scale degrees, numbered one through seven. Each degree has a traditional function name:

- Degree 1 - the tonic.
- Degree 2 - the supertonic.
- Degree 3 - the mediant.
- Degree 4 - the subdominant.
- Degree 5 - the dominant.
- Degree 6 - the submediant.
- Degree 7 - the leading tone.

### Movable-do solfege

Using movable-do solfege, the seven degrees are sung as:

- Degree 1 - Do.
- Degree 2 - Re.
- Degree 3 - Mi.
- Degree 4 - Fa.
- Degree 5 - So.
- Degree 6 - La.
- Degree 7 - Ti.

"Movable" means Do is always the tonic of whatever key you are in, rather than a fixed pitch.

### The church modes per degree

Each scale degree names a church mode, the mode you get by treating that degree as the starting note while keeping the same seven pitches of the parent major scale:

- Degree 1 - Ionian (this is the ordinary major scale).
- Degree 2 - Dorian.
- Degree 3 - Phrygian.
- Degree 4 - Lydian.
- Degree 5 - Mixolydian.
- Degree 6 - Aeolian (this is the natural minor scale).
- Degree 7 - Locrian.

### Triad qualities

A triad is a three-note chord. Its quality describes the sizes of the intervals stacked inside it:

- Major triad - a bright-sounding triad (a major third on the bottom, a minor third on top).
- Minor triad - a darker-sounding triad (a minor third on the bottom, a major third on top).
- Diminished triad - a tense, unstable triad (two minor thirds stacked, spanning a diminished fifth).

### Seventh-chord qualities

A seventh chord is a four-note chord, a triad with one more third stacked on top:

- Major seventh - a major triad with a major seventh above the root. This is nicknamed "Delta" (the trainer writes it with the symbol that looks like a triangle, the Greek capital letter Delta). Spoken aloud, always say "major seventh."
- Dominant seventh - a major triad with a minor seventh above the root.
- Minor seventh - a minor triad with a minor seventh above the root.
- Half-diminished seventh - a diminished triad with a minor seventh above the root. The trainer writes it with the symbol that is a small circle with a slash through it (an "o with a slash"). Spoken aloud, always say "half-diminished seventh."

### Inversions and their figured-bass nicknames

An inversion describes which chord tone is sitting in the bass (the lowest voice). Inverting a chord does not change its root or its quality; only the bass note changes. The traditional figured-bass nicknames are:

For triads (three-note chords):

- Root position - the root is in the bass. Spoken: "root position." No figure is written.
- First inversion - the third is in the bass. Figure: the numeral six. Spoken: "first inversion," also called the "six" chord.
- Second inversion - the fifth is in the bass. Figure: six over four. Spoken: "second inversion," also called the "six-four" chord.

For seventh chords (four-note chords):

- Root position - the root is in the bass. Figure: the numeral seven. Spoken: "root position," also called the "seven" chord.
- First inversion - the third is in the bass. Figure: six over five. Spoken: "first inversion," also called the "six-five" chord.
- Second inversion - the fifth is in the bass. Figure: four over three. Spoken: "second inversion," also called the "four-three" chord.
- Third inversion - the seventh is in the bass. Figure: four over two. Spoken: "third inversion," also called the "four-two" chord.

### Quartal chords

Ordinary chords are built by stacking thirds. Quartal chords are built by stacking perfect fourths instead, so they have no traditional major or minor quality:

- Quartal triad - a three-note chord made of two stacked perfect fourths. The trainer labels it with a lowercase "q."
- Quartal seventh - a four-note chord made of three stacked perfect fourths. The trainer labels it with "q4."

### Roman-numeral conventions

The trainer labels each chord with a Roman numeral whose form encodes the quality:

- An uppercase Roman numeral (for example I, IV, V) means a major-ish chord (a major triad, a major seventh, or a dominant seventh).
- A lowercase Roman numeral (for example ii, iii, vi) means a minor-ish chord (a minor triad or a minor seventh).
- A small circle "o" appended to the numeral means a diminished triad.
- A small circle with a slash through it appended to the numeral means a half-diminished seventh.
- The triangle "Delta" appended to the numeral means a major seventh.
- The small numbers attached to a numeral are figured-bass inversion figures; they tell you the inversion (see above). Here they are written in caret and underscore notation: caret-six means the figure six, caret-six underscore-four means the figure six-four, caret-seven means seven, caret-six underscore-five means six-five, caret-four underscore-three means four-three, and caret-four underscore-two means four-two.

## The shapes

The trainer drills nine "hand shapes." Each shape is a recipe of stacked intervals (thirds, fourths) and an inversion. Within a single shape, every column slides the same shape up to a different scale degree.

- Shape 33 - a root-position triad: two stacked thirds, root in the bass.
- Shape 34 - a first-inversion triad: a triad with its third in the bass (the "six").
- Shape 43 - a second-inversion triad: a triad with its fifth in the bass (the "six-four").
- Shape 44 - a quartal triad: two stacked perfect fourths.
- Shape 333 - a root-position seventh chord: three stacked thirds, root in the bass.
- Shape 332 - a first-inversion seventh chord: a seventh chord with its third in the bass (the "six-five").
- Shape 323 - a second-inversion seventh chord: a seventh chord with its fifth in the bass (the "four-three").
- Shape 233 - a third-inversion seventh chord: a seventh chord with its seventh in the bass (the "four-two").
- Shape 444 - a quartal seventh chord: three stacked perfect fourths.
